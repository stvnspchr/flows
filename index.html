Three Years of Extremes on the Platte

When our team set up the first few timelapse cameras in the central Platte valley at the head of 2011, we thought we would be lucky to capture a few abnormally wet and dry years over the next decade. To our surprise, we recorded a wet and dry year back to back. 2013 proved to be another exceptional year.

CFS is the measurement by which water flow is measured. It means cubic feet per second, a measure of volume per time. 200 cfs would mean that 200 cubic feet of water is passing a given point every second.

A good rule of thumb to help visualize cubic feet per second is to think of one cfs as a basketball. Imagine 500 basketballs rolling past your feet in one second; that is 500 cfs. Each stream looks different at the same cfs. For a small creek, 500 cfs would seem like a raging river. For a larger, wider river, 500 cfs would seem like a trickle.

In 2011, record snowpack melt filled the Platte River with water. Near Kearney NE, the central Platte River averaged around 6,000 cfs, or 6,000 rolling basketballs per second.

Almost exaclty one year later, the central Platte River near Kearney NE was flowing at 10 cfs, virtually dry.

In July of 2013, there was 80 cfs in the central Platte River. Just two months later, flood waters from Colorado's Front Range would rapidly swell the river, exceeding 2011 levels. Several decade long records on the Platte River were matched and overtaken during this flood event.

The last three years on the Platte River in south central Nebraska have demonstrated two extremes. 2011 was a major water year for the Platte Basin while 2012 and 2013 were both dry years.
